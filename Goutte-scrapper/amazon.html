Product description

51st state is a card game in which players control one of four powers trying to build a new country. Players put new locations into play, and send people to work in buildings to gain resources and new skills. 51st state: master set marks the rebirth of the 51st state line, with this set containing 88 cards from the original base game, and 50 cards each from both the new era and winter expansions. The entire set has been rebalanced to offer a cohesive experience no matter which expansion you choose to use.

Martin Gonzalvez
5.0 out of 5 starsA satisfyingly crunchy, medium-weight, card-driven area control and worker-placement game
March 5, 2018
51st State is a medium-weight strategy game for 1-4 players (yes, solo play rules are included, for when your gaming group bails on you) that plays in about 60-90 minutes. This post-apocalyptic game was first released in 2010 to little acclaim. Undaunted, the designer took the same system and built another game, Imperial Settlers, with a lighter theme, that became a success. That allowed the designer to revisit and refine his earlier 51st State design by re-releasing it in 2016, as a Master Set, with two expansions (Winter and New Era) included in the box. So there is a lot of thought, craft, history and most importantly, brilliant game play inside this box.

In 51st State, America is a barren, post-apocalyptic land. There is no central government, no organized society. Think Mad Max -- when we first played it, we had the soundtrack to Mad Max: Fury Road playing in the background to provide the proper ambiance. Players take on the role of one of four Factions vying to unite the various communities and roving bands into their Faction in this harsh, hard-scrabble new world, and achieve dominance as the 51st State. The cards represent Locations that your scouts have discovered in the world, and that could potentially be added to increase your state's economy. One cool thing about this game is that you can use the cards in different ways.

1) You can BUILD a card, which means you add that Location to your Faction's state. That Location will then generate benefits for your state every turn, in the form of resources (like fuel, iron, bricks, and guns), extra features, and actions.

2) If you don't have the construction resources to build a Location, you might still be able to strike a DEAL with that card. You will still earn benefits from that card each round.

3) Sometimes, you will need a lot of resources in a hurry. If you already have some locations built and added to your state, you might decide that the time has come to burn down a Location that has outlived its usefulness -- this is a harsh world, after all. In that case, you can RAZE a Location to gain a lot of resources all at once.

4) No post-apocalyptic game would be complete without the ability to inflict hurt on your rival factions. If you have enough guns and goons, you can RAZE your opponents' Locations, thus depriving your rivals of resources, and taking them for yourself. Beware, they can and will do the same to you.

5) Another way that you can exploit your rivals is by placing one of your workers on their Production cards, thus using your rivals' resources for your benefit. Less violent, but still an unwelcome development.

This game provides no less than seven options of actions that players can perform during the Action phase, so there is a lot of opportunity to develop your unique winning strategy. Each Faction has different strengths and weaknesses that force you to change your play style depending on the Faction you're playing. For example, the Merchants Faction excels at striking deals, while the Mutants Faction is low on construction resources, but has a lot of guns to raze your locations.

Once a Faction reaches 25 Victory Points, that triggers the end game sequence. The Faction with the most Locations and Victory Points at the end of the game wins. In our first game, my daughter defeated me in a close, tense match, 43-41. Grrr, now I can't wait to take my revenge!

Bottom line: this is a satisfyingly crunchy, medium-weight, card driven strategy game with a nice range of mechanics: Area Control, Worker Placement, and Economic Engine-Building. The included rules do a pretty good job of explaining game flow. Just don't mind the oddly forced attempts at humor within. In about 30 minutes, my daughter and I went from not knowing what we were doing, to being fully absorbed and entertained by this well-designed, polished, and reasonably-priced gem of a game.
